Why Welsh Rugby Still Matters More Than Ever
✎ By John James | 📅 10 May 2026 | ⏱ 4 min read

Welsh rugby has taken some heavy hits in recent years.

Poor results, uncertainty off the field, financial pressure, regional criticism and frustration from supporters have all combined to create a difficult period for the game in Wales. Social media has often turned toxic, and many fans are asking the same question: "Where do we go from here?"

But despite everything, Welsh rugby still matters deeply to the people of Wales — perhaps now more than ever.

Because Welsh rugby has never just been about winning trophies.

It is about identity. It is about communities, valleys, towns and cities coming together behind one jersey. It is fathers and daughters travelling across the country for away games. It is pubs packed before kick-off. It is singing the anthem with goosebumps on your arms.

Few nations in world rugby live and breathe the sport quite like Wales.

And history shows that Welsh rugby has always been at its strongest when people write it off.

The current generation of young players coming through gives genuine reasons for optimism. Across the URC and age-grade rugby, there are talented players beginning to emerge who could form the next core of the national side over the next 5–10 years.

It will not happen overnight. There is no magic fix. But rebuilding properly could actually create a stronger long-term future for Welsh rugby than constantly papering over the cracks.

Supporters also have a role to play. Criticism is part of sport and always will be, but there is a difference between demanding better and completely turning on the game itself. Welsh rugby needs energy, support and belief as much as it needs structural improvements.

The global rugby landscape is changing rapidly too. The growth of rugby in new markets, bigger competitions, streaming platforms and international exposure means there are still huge opportunities for Wales to remain an important rugby nation moving forward.

And one thing history tells us about Welsh sport: never underestimate Welsh resilience.

The red jersey still means something.
The anthem still means something.
The passion is still there.

Welsh rugby is down. But it is not finished.

There are few sporting experiences quite like a major rugby day in Wales. When Welsh rugby is alive, the atmosphere becomes something special.

Principality Stadium — Cardiff

It starts here. When the roof is closed and the anthem begins, the Principality Stadium becomes one of the most intimidating and emotional venues in world rugby. Local Club Rugby

Sometimes the best rugby atmosphere is not in a giant stadium at all. Small Welsh rugby clubs still represent the heart of the game. That grassroots passion is where Welsh rugby was built.

Scarlets on a Big European Night

Welsh regional rugby has had difficult moments, but Llanelli on a major European night can still produce incredible energy. When the crowd gets behind the team, Parc y Scarlets becomes a very difficult place for visiting sides.

Cardiff Derbies

Local rivalries still matter hugely in Welsh rugby culture. Whether regional or club level, derby games bring emotion, intensity and unpredictability that supporters love.

Away Days

Welsh rugby fans are also famous for travelling brilliantly. Whether in Dublin, Paris, London or Edinburgh, Welsh supporters often create some of the loudest and most colourful atmospheres anywhere in rugby.

At its core, Welsh rugby has always been powered by people.

And no matter how difficult results become, the atmosphere surrounding the game in Wales remains something genuinely special.

The Grand Slam Years

Welsh Grand Slams are the stuff of legend. The 2005, 2008, 2012 and 2019 Grand Slams showed that modern Wales could still produce moments of real glory. Each one felt like a nation exhaling years of tension and frustration in a single afternoon.

Beating England at Twickenham

Some results matter more than others. Beating England, particularly at Twickenham, holds a special place in Welsh rugby culture. The combination of history, rivalry and pride makes those victories feel disproportionately important to Welsh supporters. Few things unite Wales quite like a win over England.

Wales vs France — Epic Six Nations Battles

Wales and France have produced some of the most dramatic and unpredictable matches in Six Nations history. Contests full of flair, passion and late drama have created memories that live long after the final whistle.

Why These Moments Still Matter

In difficult periods for Welsh rugby, these memories serve an important purpose. They remind supporters what is possible. They remind players what the jersey represents. They prove that Wales, at its best, can compete with anyone in the world.

History cannot be taken away.

And Welsh rugby has produced enough of it to fuel belief through even the hardest of times.

The URC: Is It Good Enough for Welsh Rugby?
✎ By John James | 📅 19 May 2026 | ⏱ 5 min read

The United Rugby Championship is the weekly bread and butter of professional rugby in Wales. Four regions — Scarlets, Ospreys, Cardiff Rugby and the Dragons — compete in a cross-border league involving South African, Irish, Scottish and Italian sides.

But is the URC actually serving Welsh rugby well? It is a question worth asking honestly.

The Case For the URC

The competition has brought genuine variety and exposure. Playing against South African franchises has raised the physical intensity. European nights still produce meaningful occasions. And for Welsh players, regular top-level competition is essential for national team development.

The URC also provides a pathway. Welsh regions can still qualify for European Champions Cup rugby, which remains the pinnacle of club competition in the northern hemisphere.

The Concerns

The honest concern is consistency. Welsh regions have struggled in the URC in recent seasons — and when the regions struggle, the national player pool suffers. Confidence built at club level feeds into international performance. When that pipeline is weak, Wales feel it in Six Nations and World Cup campaigns.

There are also questions about resources. Irish provinces in particular have significant financial advantages that make direct competition difficult. Keeping Welsh talent at home rather than losing it to better-funded clubs abroad remains an ongoing challenge.

What Needs to Improve

Welsh regions need sustained investment, smart recruitment and stable coaching environments to be genuinely competitive in the URC. That is easier said than done given the financial landscape, but the connection between regional success and national team quality cannot be ignored.

More competitive Welsh regions would mean more confident Welsh players heading into international windows — and a stronger national team as a result.

The URC is the right competition for Welsh rugby to be in.

But being in it is not enough. Wales needs its regions to be genuinely competing — not just participating.

Grassroots Rugby in Wales: The Heartbeat Nobody Talks About
✎ By John James | 📅 20 May 2026 | ⏱ 4 min read

Every time Wales runs out at Principality Stadium, something much more important happened first.

A child turned up to a muddy pitch on a Saturday morning. A volunteer coach gave up a weekend. A club ran a fundraiser to keep the lights on. A family drove across the county to watch an under-11s game in the pouring rain.

Grassroots rugby in Wales is the foundation that the entire professional game is built on. And it does not get nearly enough credit.

The Club Network

Wales has one of the densest rugby club networks of any nation in the world relative to its population. From Wrexham to Swansea, from Aberystwyth to Cardiff, hundreds of clubs at all levels keep the sport alive week in and week out. These clubs are community institutions as much as they are sports organisations — places where friendships are formed, rivalries are built and lifelong supporters are created.

Where Welsh Players Start

Almost every Welsh international started at a local club. The talent pathway runs from grassroots through schools, through age-grade regional rugby and eventually into professional contracts. But without that first step — without those clubs existing and thriving — the pathway breaks before it even begins.

Clubs that are well-run, well-funded and well-attended create an environment where talented youngsters want to stay in Wales and develop. Clubs that are struggling have the opposite effect.

The Volunteers Who Make It Happen

Perhaps the most under-appreciated people in Welsh rugby are the volunteers. Coaches, committee members, kit washers, pitch markers, fixture secretaries — hundreds of thousands of hours of unpaid work goes into keeping Welsh rugby clubs running every single year. These are the real backbone of the sport in Wales.

If you care about the Welsh national team, support your local club.

Because without grassroots rugby, there is no Welsh rugby at all.

Six Nations: Why It Still Matters More Than Anything Else
✎ By John James | 📅 21 May 2026 | ⏱ 4 min read

You can talk about the World Cup. You can debate the value of the URC. You can analyse summer tours and autumn internationals all you like.

But for Welsh rugby supporters, nothing — absolutely nothing — compares to the Six Nations.

Six weeks in February and March. Six weeks where Wales matters on a world stage. Six weeks where every conversation in every pub, every workplace and every school playground in Wales comes back to the same thing: the rugby.

The History of It

The Six Nations has roots going back to 1883. More than 140 years of competition between England, Wales, Scotland, Ireland, France and Italy. The history is inseparable from the competition itself. Every game carries weight that goes beyond the present. Wales vs England is not just about this season's table — it is about more than a century of rivalry, pride and history.

Why Wales Feels It So Deeply

For a small nation of around three million people, Wales punches extraordinarily above its weight in the Six Nations. Grand Slams. Triple Crowns. Championship titles. Wales has won more than its fair share over the decades.

But it is not just about winning. Six Nations Saturdays in Wales take on a life of their own regardless of where Wales is in the table. The build-up, the nerves, the communal watching experience — whether in a stadium, a pub or a living room — is something uniquely powerful.

What It Gives Welsh Rugby

The Six Nations gives Welsh rugby its biggest audience, its biggest moments and its biggest emotional peaks. It is the competition that reminds the world Wales exists as a rugby force. And it is the competition that, even in difficult years, gives supporters something to hold onto and believe in.

The Six Nations is where Welsh rugby defines itself.

Every year. Whatever the form guide says.

The current generation of Welsh players in their early-to-mid twenties will be at the right age for 2027. This is genuinely exciting. Players gaining experience in the URC and with the national team now will have years of top-level rugby behind them by the time Australia comes around.

The honest assessment is that 2027 is a realistic target for Wales to arrive as genuine dark horses rather than also-rans. Not favourites — the All Blacks, South Africa and Ireland will likely be ahead of them in the pecking order. That is an achievable goal if the building blocks are put in place now.

There was a time not long ago when the Ospreys were the dominant force in Welsh regional rugby. Multiple Pro14 titles, European pedigree, a stadium packed with passionate supporters and a conveyor belt of Welsh internationals wearing the black jersey.

That era feels distant now. But the potential that made the Ospreys great has not disappeared.

What Made the Ospreys Special

At their peak, the Ospreys were built on a foundation of outstanding Welsh talent combined with smart recruitment. The squad had genuine depth, a clear playing style and a settled environment that allowed players to develop. Results followed naturally from that stable base.

The connection with the South Wales rugby community also gave the Ospreys an identity that felt genuine. The Swansea and Neath heartland, the Liberty Stadium atmosphere on big nights, the pride of representing a specific part of Wales — these things matter to supporters and players alike.

Recent seasons have been inconsistent. The Ospreys have shown flashes of the quality that once made them a URC force, but sustained performance has been harder to come by. The challenge of retaining top Welsh talent while also competing with the financial power of Irish and South African franchises has been real and ongoing.

But the foundations are still there. The region has the support base, the geography and the rugby tradition to be a major force again.

Why There Is Still Reason for Belief

Welsh rugby needs strong regions. The Ospreys, historically, have been its strongest. Getting the Ospreys back to genuine URC competitiveness would have a direct positive effect on Welsh international rugby — producing more confident, more experienced players heading into national camps.

The sleeping giant of South Wales rugby has woken before. There is every reason to believe it can wake again.

The Ospreys are not a finished story.

They are a chapter waiting to be written again.

If you want to understand where Welsh rugby is headed, do not just look at the Six Nations table. Look at the Under-20s.

The Wales Under-20s team is where the next generation of Welsh internationals is being forged. These are the players who, within five years, could be pulling on the senior red jersey. The performances, the development and the environment at Under-20 level have a direct impact on what Welsh rugby looks like at the 2027 World Cup and beyond.

Why the Under-20s Matter So Much

International rugby at Under-20 level — including the Six Nations Under-20s and the World Rugby Under-20 Championship — is the most important development competition in the world game. The teams that consistently produce strong Under-20 squads tend to be the teams that build sustainable international success over time.

Ireland's dominance at Under-20 level in the 2010s and early 2020s directly fed into their senior team's extraordinary rise. The connection is not coincidence. Develop players well at age-grade level and the senior team benefits for a decade.

What Wales Needs From the Under-20s Programme

First, consistency of selection and coaching. Young players need a stable environment where they know what is expected of them and can develop without constant disruption. Second, playing time. Wales Under-20 players need to be getting genuine game time at their regions — not sitting on benches waiting for opportunities that never come.

As we discussed in our article on building for the 2027 World Cup, the players in their late teens and early twenties right now are exactly the right age to be the core of that tournament squad. The investment made in them today is the investment Wales makes in its own future.

Following the Under-20s

I watch the Wales Under-20s with the same investment I watch the senior side. Sometimes more, because you are watching potential take shape in real time. You see a young flanker dominate a game and think — that could be a future Wales captain. You see a fly-half orchestrate a comeback and wonder how long before they are starting at Principality Stadium.

That is one of the genuine joys of following Welsh rugby at every level. The story never ends. There is always a next chapter being written somewhere.

The Under-20s are not the future of Welsh rugby.

They are the present of Welsh rugby — happening right now, if you are watching.

The Principality Stadium: More Than Just a Rugby Ground
✎ By John James | 📅 24 May 2026 | ⏱ 4 min read

There is a moment that happens inside the Principality Stadium before every Wales home match. The teams are in the tunnel. The crowd is at full noise. And then the anthem starts.

Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau — Land of My Fathers. Sung by 74,500 people with a conviction that makes the hairs stand up on the back of your neck, however many times you have heard it. That moment is not just a rugby ritual. It is a statement of identity from a small nation that refuses to be overlooked.

The Principality Stadium is where that statement is made most loudly. And it is far more than a rugby ground.

The History of the Place

The current stadium was built for the 1999 Rugby World Cup, replacing the old Cardiff Arms Park terraces that had stood as the home of Welsh rugby for over a century. The Arms Park itself still exists alongside it — home to Cardiff Rugby — but the national stadium rose around and above it, becoming one of the most recognisable sporting venues in the world.

The retractable roof was groundbreaking when it opened. Closing that roof in bad weather transforms the noise inside into something almost physical — a wall of sound that has driven Wales to victories that seemed impossible and broken the confidence of visiting sides who had no idea what was coming.

The Matches That Made It Legendary

Every Welsh rugby supporter carries a mental highlight reel from this ground. Grand Slam clinchers. Last-minute wins against England. Unforgettable European nights. The stadium has hosted Rugby World Cup matches, FA Cup finals, boxing world title fights and concerts from the biggest acts on the planet — but it is on rugby internationals that it truly comes alive.

As we described in our piece on the greatest atmospheres in Welsh rugby, when the roof is closed and Wales are playing England, the Principality Stadium is arguably the most intense sporting environment in the world.

What It Means to a Welsh Rugby Fan

I have been lucky enough to experience match days at the Principality Stadium many times over the years. Each one is different. Each one carries its own emotion depending on what is at stake. But the constant — the thing that never changes — is the feeling of being part of something much bigger than yourself.

That is what this stadium does. It collects the passion of an entire nation and holds it in one place for eighty minutes. It turns individual supporters into a single collective voice. It makes you feel, regardless of the result, that being Welsh and loving rugby is something worth being enormously proud of.

The Principality Stadium is not just where Wales plays rugby.

It is where Wales reminds the world who it is.
